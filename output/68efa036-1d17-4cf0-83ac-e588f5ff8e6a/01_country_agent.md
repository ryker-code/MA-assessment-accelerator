### Currency & Capital
Pakistan allows profit repatriation under the Companies Act, 2017, but requires approval from the State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) for large outward remittances. Capital controls exist for foreign exchange transactions, particularly for current account conversions.

### Risk Flags
- High inflation (7.2% in 2024)
- Currency depreciation (PKR/USD rose to 179.5 in 2024)
- Political instability and policy uncertainty
- Capital controls on foreign exchange repatriation

### Narrative Summary
Pakistan's economy faces significant macroeconomic challenges, including persistent inflation (7.2% in 2024) and a depreciating rupee (PKR/USD 179.5 in 2024), driven by fiscal imbalances and external financing needs. While the IMF's 37-month EFF program has stabilized the economy, growth remains subdued at 2.5% in 2024, with structural weaknesses in public finances and private sector credit constraints. Political instability and regulatory uncertainties pose risks to foreign investors. The corporate tax rate of 29% and capital controls on currency repatriation add complexity to M&A transactions. However, the telecom sector, including Telenor Pakistan, benefits from growing digital adoption and government initiatives to modernize infrastructure, though regulatory oversight remains a critical consideration.
