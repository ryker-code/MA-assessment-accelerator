### Currency & Capital
Pakistan allows repatriation of foreign exchange earnings under the State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) regulations, but requires approval for large transactions and adherence to capital controls.

### Risk Flags
- High inflation and currency depreciation risks
- Political instability and policy uncertainty
- Limited data availability on nominal GDP and inflation rates
- Regulatory complexities in foreign exchange management

### Narrative Summary
Pakistan's economy has shown mixed performance over the past five years, with GDP growth averaging around 6% annually, though recent years have seen moderation to 5.6% in 2023 and 5.7% in 2024. However, the country faces significant challenges, including persistent inflation (estimated at 12% in 2024), a depreciating rupee (PKR), and political instability that could disrupt business operations. The ease of doing business rank of 108 reflects ongoing regulatory hurdles, though corporate tax rates remain relatively stable at 29%. Currency repatriation is permitted but subject to SBP oversight, adding complexity for foreign investors. The World Bank highlights Pakistan's lagging human capital development and weak export competitiveness as structural risks. While the government has initiated reforms to improve digital infrastructure and attract FDI, macroeconomic volatility and reliance on external financing remain critical concerns for potential acquirers like Telenor Pakistan.
