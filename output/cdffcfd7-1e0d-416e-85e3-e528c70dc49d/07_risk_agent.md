### Risk Details

#### Post-Acquisition Leverage Overload
- **Category:** CROSS_WORKSTREAM
- **Severity:** HIGH | **Probability:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Bharti Airtel already carries USD 12.5bn in net debt and faces significant spectrum liabilities in India. Acquiring a massive infrastructure-heavy entity like BT (which manages the Openreach network) would likely result in a combined leverage ratio that could breach debt covenants or trigger credit rating downgrades.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Utilize equity financing or strategic partners (e.g., Singtel) to fund the acquisition
  - Divest non-core BT assets post-acquisition to deleverage

#### Dual-Jurisdiction Regulatory Bottleneck
- **Category:** CROSS_WORKSTREAM
- **Severity:** MEDIUM | **Probability:** HIGH
- **Description:** The deal requires simultaneous approval from India's Department of Telecommunications (DoT) for outbound investment and the UK's antitrust and national security regulators (given BT's critical infrastructure status). The misalignment of these two regulatory timelines could lead to significant deal slippage.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Engage early with both UK and Indian regulators
  - Include flexible long-stop dates in the Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA)

#### Currency Translation and Earnings Drag
- **Category:** CROSS_WORKSTREAM
- **Severity:** MEDIUM | **Probability:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Airtel reports in INR while BT generates revenue in GBP. While the GBP is currently stable, any significant depreciation of the GBP against the INR would create a translation loss on the consolidated income statement, eroding the perceived value of the diversification strategy.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Implement a robust FX hedging strategy for GBP/INR
  - Utilize natural hedging by financing the acquisition through GBP-denominated debt

#### UK Market Share Erosion
- **Category:** MARKET
- **Severity:** HIGH | **Probability:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** BT's 35% market share is under threat from aggressive full-fiber expansion by CityFibre and Virgin Media O2. Airtel's entry into a 'three-network scenario' may not provide the stability they seek if BT's dominance continues to decline.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Accelerate BT's full-fiber rollout using Airtel's operational efficiencies
  - Focus on B2B/Enterprise segments where BT has a stronger moat

#### Critical Infrastructure Compliance Costs
- **Category:** REGULATORY
- **Severity:** MEDIUM | **Probability:** HIGH
- **Description:** The UK's National Emergency Telecommunications Plan (2024) imposes strict mandates on caller location data and emergency services, increasing operational compliance costs for the network owner.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Allocate specific CapEx for regulatory compliance upgrades
  - Leverage BT's existing compliance framework

#### Indian Spectrum Liability Constraints
- **Category:** COMPANY
- **Severity:** MEDIUM | **Probability:** HIGH
- **Description:** Airtel's ongoing high capital expenditure for 5G and spectrum payment liabilities in India may limit the liquid capital available for the integration and modernization of BT's legacy systems.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Restructure Indian spectrum payment schedules
  - Secure external bridge financing for the UK integration phase

#### Post-Brexit Regulatory Divergence
- **Category:** GEOPOLITICAL
- **Severity:** LOW | **Probability:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Ongoing adjustments to UK trade and regulatory frameworks post-Brexit may create unforeseen operational frictions for a non-EU/non-UK parent company.
- **Mitigants:**
  - Maintain a strong local UK management team to navigate domestic policy

### Cross-Workstream Insights
- The strategic drive for diversification is countered by a 'leverage trap' where Airtel's existing Indian debt combined with BT's infrastructure costs creates a high-risk financial profile.
- The deal's success is disproportionately dependent on the synchronization of two very different regulatory regimes (India's DoT and UK's National Security/Antitrust), creating a high probability of timing risk.
- Airtel is essentially trading a duopolistic struggle in India (vs Jio) for a fragmented, high-competition fiber war in the UK, meaning the 'diversification' benefit may be offset by similar competitive pressures.
