### Currency & Capital
Pakistan allows repatriation of profits and capital under certain conditions, but foreign investors must comply with regulations set by the State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) and the Board of Investment (BOI). Repatriation of foreign exchange is permitted for dividends, interest, and capital gains, but it is subject to approval and documentation.

### Risk Flags
- High inflation rates impacting purchasing power
- Political instability and policy uncertainty
- Currency depreciation risks
- Regulatory complexity for foreign investment

### Narrative Summary
Pakistan's economy has shown mixed performance over the past five years, with GDP growth fluctuating between 2.4% in 2020 and 7.1% in 2022, followed by a slowdown to 5.6% in 2024. Despite this, the country faces significant macroeconomic challenges, including high inflation (24% in 2024), political instability, and a depreciating currency (PKR). The ease of doing business in Pakistan is ranked 108 out of 190 countries, indicating moderate challenges for foreign investors. Corporate tax rates are relatively high at 29%, and while repatriation of profits is permitted, it is subject to regulatory oversight. The country's economic performance has lagged behind regional peers, with weak export growth and declining competitiveness. These factors pose notable risks for foreign investors, particularly in the telecommunications sector, where Telenor Pakistan operates. However, opportunities exist in a growing digital economy and potential policy reforms aimed at improving the business environment.
